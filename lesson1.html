Major and Minor Triads

Lesson 1

Objective

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to identify major and minor triads by listening, using both analytical (note-by-note) and intuitive (holistic sound) strategies.

What Is a Triad?

A triad consists of three notes: root, third, and fifth. The difference between major and minor lies in the distance between the root and the third.

Major and Minor Triads

  • A major triad contains a larger interval (major third) above the root and is typically perceived as bright and stable.
  • A minor triad contains a smaller interval (minor third) and is often perceived as darker or more tense.

Guided Listening Sequence

Audio Placement

Sound 1A
Root note → major third (played separately, ascending)
Sound 1B
Root note → minor third (played separately, ascending)

Listen to each pair of notes. Focus on the distance between them.

  • Does the second note feel farther away or closer?
  • How does the second note change the character of the first?

Building the Triad Step-by-Step

Audio Placement

For each example:

Sound 2A
Root alone
Sound 2B
Root + third (harmonic or melodic)
Sound 2C
Full triad (root, third, fifth together)

As you listen:

  • Notice how adding the third changes the sound.
  • Then notice how the full chord feels when complete.
  • Ask: does the final chord sound bright or dark?

Direct Comparison

Audio Placement

Sound 3A
Major triad
Sound 3B
Minor triad (same root)
Play Both
Compare back-to-back
  • Which sounds brighter?
  • Which sounds more subdued?
  • Focus on the overall quality rather than individual notes.

Reflection Before Identification

Audio Placement

For each trial:

Sound 4A
Three notes played separately
Sound 4B
Same notes as chord

Before answering:

  • Recall how the second note related to the first.
  • Connect that to how the full chord sounds.
  • Make a judgment: major or minor.

Listening Strategy for Learners

  • Start with the interval (first two notes).
  • Then confirm with the full chord sound.
  • Trust your first impression when possible.
  • Use consistent mental labels like bright vs. dark to reinforce memory.
