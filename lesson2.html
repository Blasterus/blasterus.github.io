Augmented Triads

Lesson 2

Objective

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to identify augmented triads by listening and distinguish them from major and minor triads.

What Is an Augmented Triad?

An augmented triad consists of:

  • A root
  • A major third
  • An augmented fifth (a raised fifth)

Compared to a major triad, only one note changes: the fifth is raised slightly. This small change creates a distinctly different sound.

Sound Characteristics

Augmented triads are often described as:

  • Unstable
  • Tense
  • Dreamlike or ambiguous
  • Floating or unresolved

Unlike major (stable) or minor (grounded but darker), augmented chords often feel like they are leaning forward or lacking a clear resolution.

Guided Listening Sequence

Altered Fifth

Sound 1A
Root → perfect fifth (separate notes)
Sound 1B
Root → augmented fifth (separate notes)
  • How far apart do the notes feel?
  • Does the second version feel slightly higher or more tense?

Major vs. Augmented

Sound 2A
Major triad (root, major third, perfect fifth)
Sound 2B
Augmented triad (same root and third, raised fifth)
Play Both
Compare back-to-back
  • The first should sound stable.
  • The second may sound more tense or unresolved.
  • Focus on how the top note changes the overall color.

Building the Augmented Triad

Sound 3A
Root alone
Sound 3B
Root + major third
Sound 3C
Full augmented triad
  • Notice that the first two notes sound like a major chord beginning.
  • Pay attention to how adding the final note changes the stability.
  • Ask: does the chord now feel complete, or unsettled?

Triad Comparison (Major, Minor, Augmented)

Purpose: Strengthen discrimination across all three types.

Sound 4A
Major triad
Sound 4B
Minor triad
Sound 4C
Augmented triad (same root)

Instructions to learner: Listen to all three:

  • Which sounds brightest and most stable? (major)
  • Which sounds darker? (minor)
  • Which sounds most tense or unresolved? (augmented)

Reflection Before Identification

Purpose: Prepare learners for independent recognition.

For each trial:

Sound 5A
Notes played separately
Sound 5B
Full chord
  • Does the chord sound stable (major), dark (minor), or tense/unresolved (augmented)?
  • Did the final note make the chord feel complete or unsettled?

Listening Strategy for Learners

  • First, identify if the chord sounds stable or unstable.
  • If stable, decide between major and minor.
  • If clearly unstable or unresolved, consider augmented.
  • Pay special attention to the top note and how it affects the chord.
