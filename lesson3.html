Diminished Triads

Lesson 3

Objective

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to identify diminished triads by listening and distinguish them from major, minor, and augmented triads.

What Is a Diminished Triad?

A diminished triad consists of:

  • A root
  • A minor third
  • A diminished fifth (a lowered fifth)

Compared to a minor triad, only one note changes: the fifth is lowered. This creates a more compressed and unstable sound.

Sound Characteristics

Diminished triads are often described as:

  • Very tense
  • Unstable
  • Compressed or tight
  • Dramatic or unsettling

Compared to:

  • Minor (dark but grounded)
  • Augmented (open but unstable)

Diminished chords tend to feel more constrained and intense.

Guided Listening Sequence

Sound 1A
Root → perfect fifth (separate notes)
Sound 1B
Root → diminished fifth (separate notes)

Instructions to learner: Listen to each pair:

  • How does the distance between the notes change?
  • Does the second version feel closer or more compressed?
  • Does it sound more severe?

Minor vs. Diminished (Single Change)

Sound 2A
Minor triad (root, minor third, perfect fifth)
Sound 2B
Diminished triad (same root and third, lowered fifth)
Play Both
Compare back-to-back
  • The first should sound dark but stable.
  • The second should sound more tense or unstable.
  • Focus on how the top note changes the overall character.

Building the Diminished Triad

  • Notice that the first two notes resemble a minor chord.
  • Pay attention to how the final note changes the sound.
  • Ask: does the chord feel stable or especially tense?

Triad Comparison (All Four Types)

Purpose: Strengthen discrimination across all learned chord types.

Sound 4A
Major triad
Sound 4B

Listen to all four:

  • Which sounds most stable? (major)
  • Which sounds darker but still grounded? (minor)
  • Which sounds open but unstable? (augmented)
  • Which sounds most tense or compressed? (diminished)

Reflection Before Identification

For each trial:

Sound 5A
Notes played separately
Sound 5B
Full chord

Before answering:

  • Did the chord sound especially tense or unstable?
  • Did it feel more compressed than a minor chord?
  • Can you identify the lowered fifth from the isolated notes?

Listening Strategy for Learners

  • First, decide if the chord feels stable or unstable.
  • If highly tense and compressed, consider diminished.
  • Compare mentally to minor: does this feel darker and more unstable?
  • Use both the isolated notes and the full chord to confirm.

Practice Guidance

Learners often confuse diminished with minor at first because both contain a minor third.

Emphasize:

  • The diminished fifth as the defining feature
  • The increased tension and instability
  • Repeated comparison with minor triads
